What is Dark Matter?

Dark matter is an invisible substance that makes up 85% of all matter in the universe. We can't see it directly, but we know it exists because of its gravitational effects on visible matter like stars and galaxies. Without dark matter, galaxies would fly apart and the universe would be smooth and uniform—and we wouldn't exist.

Galaxies form and stay stable. The universe has rich structure with galaxy clusters, walls, and filaments.

Universe WITHOUT Dark Matter

Galaxies fly apart. The universe remains smooth and uniform with very little structure.

How We Detect Dark Matter

🌌
Galaxy Rotation

Galaxies spin faster than they should based on visible matter. Extra gravity from dark matter holds them together.

🔍
Gravitational Lensing

Dark matter's gravity bends light from distant stars, creating a "fishbowl" distortion effect we can measure.

📡
Cosmic Microwave Background

Faint light from just after the Big Bang shows patterns that reveal dark matter existed very early in the universe.

Leading Theories

WIMPs

Weakly Interacting Massive Particles with about 100 times the mass of protons. They would complete the theory of supersymmetry.

Losing traction
Axions

Extremely light particles—a trillion times lighter than an electron. They behave more like waves than particles.

Gaining interest
Dark Sector

An entire world of matter existing alongside ours, with "dark" versions of atoms and photons that rarely interact with us.

Under study
Primordial Black Holes

Black holes formed just after the Big Bang that have been wandering the universe ever since, creating gravitational effects.

Gaining traction

Discovery Timeline

1930s

Fritz Zwicky's Observation

Astronomer Fritz Zwicky noticed galaxy clusters were spinning faster than they should based on visible matter. He coined the term "dark matter."

1970s

Vera Rubin's Discovery

Astronomer Vera Rubin found that galaxies were rotating so fast they would fly apart unless something invisible was holding them together.

1985

WIMP Theory Proposed

UChicago Prof. Michael Turner co-coined the term "WIMPs" (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles) as a leading dark matter candidate.

Present

Ongoing Experiments

Scientists worldwide use advanced detectors deep underground, particle accelerators, and cosmic surveys to hunt for dark matter.
